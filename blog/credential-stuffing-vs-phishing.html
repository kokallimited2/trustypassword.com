⚔️ Credential Stuffing vs Phishing: Understand Both Threats

By Sophie Laurent, Cybersecurity Awareness Trainer, Trusty Password · 14 Apr 2026 · 3 min read · 524 words

On this page

  • What Is Credential Stuffing?
  • What Is Phishing?
  • Key Differences
  • Why You Need to Defend Against Both

Credential stuffing and phishing are the two most common methods attackers use to take over accounts. They work differently and require different defences. Understanding both threats is essential for protecting your online accounts.

What Is Credential Stuffing?

Credential stuffing uses username and password pairs obtained from data breaches. Attackers take credentials leaked from one service — for example, a 2024 breach of a social media platform exposing 100 million email-and-password combinations — and automatically test them against dozens of other services (banking, email, shopping, streaming).

How it works:

  1. An attacker obtains a list of credentials from a data breach (available on the dark web or hacker forums).
  2. Automated bots test these credentials against other services — often hundreds of thousands of login attempts per hour.
  3. Accounts where the same email and password were reused across multiple sites are compromised simultaneously.

The key fact: Credential stuffing succeeds because of password reuse. If you use the same password on multiple sites, a breach of any one of those sites compromises all of them. The attacker does not need to trick you — your own password reuse does the work.

What Is Phishing?

Phishing uses deception to trick you into voluntarily giving your credentials to an attacker. Unlike credential stuffing, which exploits password reuse, phishing exploits human trust and attention.

How it works:

  1. An attacker sends an email, text message, or social media message pretending to be a legitimate organisation.
  2. The message contains a link to a fake login page that looks identical to the real one.
  3. You enter your username and password on the fake page, handing them directly to the attacker.

Phishing can target anyone, regardless of whether they reuse passwords. Even if every account has a unique password, a single moment of distraction on a convincing fake login page can compromise an account. Read our guide on how to detect a fake login page for detailed detection steps.

Why You Need to Defend Against Both

Unique passwords per site (using a password manager) completely neutralises credential stuffing. Even if one service is breached, your other accounts are safe because the password is different on every site.

However, unique passwords do not protect against phishing. If you enter a unique password into a fake login page, the attacker captures that unique password and can use it on the real site immediately. You need both defences:

  • A password manager to generate and store unique passwords for every account, preventing credential stuffing.
  • Phishing awareness and URL verification to ensure you only enter credentials on the real site, preventing credential theft.
