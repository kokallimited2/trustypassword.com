📧 How to Spot a Phishing Email Before You Click

By Sophie Laurent, Cybersecurity Awareness Trainer, Trusty Password · 7 May 2026 · 3 min read · 544 words

On this page

  • Check the Sender Address, Not Just the Display Name
  • Look for Generic Greetings
  • Examine the URL Before You Click
  • Watch for Urgency and Threat Language
  • Poor Grammar and Spelling
  • Unexpected Attachments
  • What to Do If You Suspect a Phishing Email

Phishing emails are the most common entry point for account takeovers, ransomware, and data breaches. In 2025, the NCSC reported that 92% of UK businesses encountered at least one phishing attempt. Learning to spot a phishing email before you click is the single most effective skill for protecting your accounts.

Check the Sender Address, Not Just the Display Name

Phishing emails often spoof the display name of a trusted organisation — "Your Bank Support" or "Amazon Security Team" — but the actual email address behind it tells a different story. Always hover over or tap the sender name to reveal the full email address. A legitimate message from your bank comes from an official domain (e.g., @natwest.com), not @natwest-security-alerts.xyz or @gmail.com.

The golden rule: If the domain after the @ symbol does not exactly match the organisation's official website domain, treat the email as suspicious.

Look for Generic Greetings

Legitimate organisations use your name in their communications. Phishing emails typically start with "Dear Customer," "Dear User," or "Dear Valued Member," because the attacker does not know your name. If an email from your bank, HMRC, or a service you use does not address you by name, that is a significant red flag.

Examine the URL Before You Click

Before clicking any link, hover over it (on desktop) or press and hold (on mobile) to preview the destination URL. The displayed link text might say "https://www.amazon.co.uk/security-alert" but the actual destination could be "amazon-security-alert.xyz/login". If the URL looks wrong, does not match the organisation, or uses an unfamiliar top-level domain (.xyz, .top, .club), do not click.

For detailed guidance on inspecting URLs, see our guide on how to inspect a URL before entering your password.

Watch for Urgency and Threat Language

Phishing emails rely on panic. Common urgency triggers include:

  • "Your account will be suspended within 24 hours"
  • "Unauthorised login detected — verify now"
  • "You have an unclaimed refund — act immediately"
  • "Security breach — reset your password immediately"

Legitimate organisations do not require you to act within hours or face consequences. If an email pressures you to act quickly, pause. Navigate directly to the organisation's website by typing the address in your browser — do not use the link in the email.

Poor Grammar and Spelling

While AI-generated phishing emails are improving, many still contain grammatical errors, awkward phrasing, or unusual word choices. Read the email critically. If a sentence sounds unnatural or contains mistakes that a legitimate organisation's communications team would catch, treat it as suspicious.

Unexpected Attachments

If an email you were not expecting contains an attachment — especially a ZIP file, PDF, DOCX, or HTML file — do not open it. Attackers use attachments to deliver malware that can steal passwords, log keystrokes, or install ransomware. Verify with the sender through a separate channel before opening any unexpected attachment.

What to Do If You Suspect a Phishing Email

  1. Do not click any links or open attachments in the suspicious email.
  2. Report it to the NCSC Suspicious Email Reporting Service at report@phishing.gov.uk (UK) or forward to your organisation's security team.
  3. Delete the email after reporting. Do not reply, unsubscribe, or interact in any way.
  4. If you already clicked a suspicious link, see our guide on what to do immediately after a phishing attack.
