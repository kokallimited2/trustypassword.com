🚨 What to Do Immediately After a Phishing Attack

By Sophie Laurent, Cybersecurity Awareness Trainer, Trusty Password · 7 Apr 2026 · 3 min read · 670 words

On this page

  • Step 1: Change the Password Immediately
  • Step 2: Revoke All Active Sessions
  • Step 3: Check and Restore Account Recovery Details
  • Step 4: Enable or Verify Multi-Factor Authentication
  • Step 5: Check for Unauthorised Actions
  • Step 6: Report the Phishing Attack
  • Step 7: Scan Your Computer for Malware
  • Preventing Future Phishing Attacks

If you think you have entered your credentials on a phishing site, act immediately. Speed matters — attackers often automate credential validation within seconds of capture. Follow these steps in order to minimise damage.

Step 1: Change the Password Immediately

Navigate directly to the real website by typing the address into your browser — do not use any link from the suspicious email or page. Log in and change your password to a new, unique, randomly generated password. If you use a password manager, generate a new password through the manager and save it immediately.

Critical: Change the password before doing anything else. Every minute counts. If the attacker has already used the captured credentials, changing the password locks them out.

Step 2: Revoke All Active Sessions

Most services allow you to see and revoke active login sessions from the security settings page. After changing your password, go to Security Settings and click "Sign out of all devices" or "Revoke all sessions." This forces any attacker who logged in before you changed the password to authenticate again — and they will not be able to because the password is now different.

This step is often overlooked but is essential because attackers may have set up persistent sessions that survive a password change on some platforms.

Step 3: Check and Restore Account Recovery Details

Attackers frequently change recovery email addresses, phone numbers, and security questions to lock you out of your own account. After changing the password:

  1. Go to Account Recovery or Security Settings.
  2. Verify that the recovery email address and phone number are yours.
  3. If they were changed, restore them to your details immediately.
  4. Check that no unrecognised MFA methods were added.

Step 4: Enable or Verify Multi-Factor Authentication

If MFA was not enabled before the phishing attack, enable it now. Use TOTP (an authenticator app) or, ideally, a passkey or hardware security key. If MFA was already enabled, verify that no unrecognised MFA methods were added by the attacker. Some phishing kits add an attacker-controlled MFA method during the attack, giving them persistent access even after the password changes.

See our guide on which MFA methods actually resist phishing for advice on choosing the right method.

Step 5: Check for Unauthorised Actions

Review the account's recent activity log if available. Look for:

  • Messages sent from your email or social media accounts (attackers often use compromised accounts to send phishing messages to your contacts).
  • Purchases or transactions you did not make — especially on shopping, banking, or payment accounts.
  • API keys or app integrations that were added without your knowledge — revoke any you do not recognise.
  • Forwarding rules added to your email (attackers set up email forwarding to receive future password reset emails).

Step 6: Report the Phishing Attack

Reporting helps protect others and may alert your organisation to a broader attack:

  • In the UK: Forward the phishing email to the NCSC Suspicious Email Reporting Service at report@phishing.gov.uk
  • If financial loss occurred: Report to Action Fraud at actionfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040
  • If financial credentials were involved: Call your bank immediately using the number on the back of your card — do not use any number from the phishing email
  • At work: Report to your IT security team immediately. Phishing attacks targeting employees can escalate to ransomware or data breaches

Step 7: Scan Your Computer for Malware

If you visited a phishing site, the page may have delivered malware in addition to capturing your credentials. Run a full antivirus scan using Windows Defender, Malwarebytes, or another trusted security tool. If you clicked a link in a phishing email that downloaded a file, disconnect from the internet before scanning.

Preventing Future Phishing Attacks

  • Use a password manager to generate and store unique passwords — it also provides domain-bound autofill that detects fake login pages.
  • Enable FIDO2/WebAuthn passkeys on accounts that support them — these cannot be phished.
  • Never click links in emails — navigate directly to websites by typing the address.
  • Learn to spot phishing emails before you click.
